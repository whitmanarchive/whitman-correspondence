535 15th St. Wash
July 29th/'74



Mr. Whitman

Dear Sir

Your letter has just been received, and thought you would like to have an early answer. I am glad to have the room as I need it and will do the best I can to accommodate you. The bedstead, springs, mattress, one feather pillow, & two hair ones, basin & pitcher the three tumblers & one small pitcher I could make use of. They are not what I should have bought out of the store

they have been used by you for some time; I have never used the room or things, if you do not ask too much for them I will take them—please write as soon as you get this & let me know what you want for them as I am anxious to have all the cleaning done before the [ &c?] return.               Besides those things there are ten chairs two old tables, rocking chair looking glass & wollen spread that I cannot use for any thing, but if you tell me what you want for them I might dispose of them for you, so please let me know their value. I will keep your other things in safty for you as you wish; and will first state here that I would not have written to worry you about the money if I had not wanted it very particularly before the &c absence I did not think it would inconvenience you to much—Please write just as soon as you get this as I will be anxious to [ heare?] your answer

Very Respectfully

Mrs. I.A. White \ 1

Correspondent:
Walt Whitman stayed with Dr. George A. White, a chiropodist, and his wife Isabella A. White from March 1, 1871, until Whitman left Washington. Whitman had paid $236 in rent through June 10, 1873 (The Library of Congress #73). On November 28, 1873, Dr. acknowledged for his wife receipt of $28 "on account . . . for rent of room etc from May 1st/73" (Charles E. Feinberg Collection). Walt Whitman gave up one room at the Whites' on June 10, 1873: "Kept the other at $2.50 a month" (The Library of Congress #68). According to a letter dated , Isabella White had written, evidently early in July, about the rent due for his room; Whitman's reply is not extant. On the 29th, White offered to purchase Walt Whitman's bedstead and certain other effects. Walt Whitman had not settled his account when White wrote again on , and offered him a credit of $10 for his furnishings against a balance of $38. These letters are in the Charles E. Feinberg Collection.(See also Walt Whitman's , letter to Peter Doyle, in which Whitman left instructions for the delivery of his boxes from the Whites.)
