July 3, 1867.



Walt Whitman Esq.

Dear Sir

While considering the question of publishing Leaves of Grass we have consulted with several eminent literary men and they have advised us most strongly not to do it.

In view of this fact, though, we are favorably impressed, as we were while we first wrote you, with the idea, we deem it injudicious to commit ourselves to its publication at the present time.

With [ the highest?] regards

[ yrs ?] Very Truly

A Simpson Co

Correspondent:
Abraham Simpson, while working for J. M. Bradstreet & Son, had supervised the binding of Drum-Taps (see Walt Whitman's , letter to Peter Eckler). Simpson wrote on , that he was going into business for himself: "Hearing you are writing another book, [I] would like to print and publish it for you and will give you better advantages than any other publishing house. . . . One of my reasons for securing your friendship is my appreciation for you as a man, with knowing your life has been devoted to help among those most in need of your assistance." On , Simpson informed Walt Whitman that "we have established a Ptg & Publishing House." But, in his , letter, he advised Walt Whitman that after consultation "with several eminent literary men, . . . though we are favorably impressed, . . . we deem it injudicious to commit ourselves to its publication at the present time."
