Copenhagen
24th of July 1876



My dear Walt Whitman.

Being totally without news from you since January this year I began to fear, that you may be severely ill; else I presume, that you would have let me hear from you, at least by papers and little parcels as ordinary I wrote to you in April a fortnight before my marriage. If the letter should not have reached you, I hereby tell you, that of the 14,th May I married my dear faithfull little wife, to whom I have been betrothed since inne years. I am a living example of the old sentence, that [ riches?] are no ingredient of happiness.

If you have dearly received my letter from April, you will know, that I have made vain and to myself very humiliating efforts to arrange a demonstration in favour of you among the [ labor?] of Copenhagen

My thoughts were with on the 4th. Your name [ most?] surely [ has?] the best glamour to be memorable at "the Centennial"; most probably it has not been [ it?] at all.

I think to write another about George Washington. Should you know one good memoirs and relations of contemporaries about the Anglo-American work?

My dear wife is greeting you as a friend. Your kind earnest [ face?] is looking on us in this moment from our wall.

Tomorrow I dare reach the age of forty years!

I should be glad if these lines may meet you in good health and good [ laughs?]

Yours

—Rudolf Schmidt

Correspondent:
The Swede Peter Carl Rudolph Schmidt (1836–1899) was the editor of the idealist For Idé og Virkelighed ("For Idea and Reality") and had translated Whitman's Democratic Vistas into Danish in 1871 (see the letter from Schmidt to Whitman of ).
