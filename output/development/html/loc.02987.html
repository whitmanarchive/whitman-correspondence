1015 O St. N.W.

Washington dc
April 27. 1890. 1



Dear Walt,

Thank you for all papers &c. that you have so kindly sent, & especially for the "Poet-Lore" with article about William,2 & the Transcript of the 19. with notice of your Lincoln Memorial evening. It is good, & I am thankful that you could go & and do it. Most noble & fitting; & I wish I could have been there.

As for me, dear Walt, I feel as if I had been more or less under a cloud, the cloud that comes of having to give too much thought & care to this world's affairs,— the [ tow?] to provide for the food, shelter & raiment of the body. My friends have been trying to get me a place in the Census Bureau, but as yet without success. I felt that I must hold on to this little home till I could see my way somewhat, & I think I have done well in so doing. I hope to be able to have a home still, & a plan is now under consideration by which I hope to have a home with a friend. If it matures I will let you know.

Soon it will be a year since William passed on! The 9th of May, so soon here now, & what a long year it has been to me; & yet in another way it seems but yesterday that I watched his last hours.— I am very sorry to learn that you have been down with the "grip," which we have had here.

You told me that you have, or can get all Magazines & papers. Have you seen the "New England Magazine" for March 1890. It has a story "A Strange Dinner Party"— by my niece, Grace Ellery Channing. If you get it, & read it, & don't care to keep it, I shall be glad of it. She is writing quite a good deal, but says that since her uncle William O'Connor died, she feels that her "literary conscience is gone." She also has published in the "Overland Monthly" but I never see that.

I will send you a little poem of hers, & will like you to return it, as I have no other of hers. It was in "Kate Field's Washington" of Feb. 12 1890.

How I wish I could run over & see you! When "my ship comes in," I will. Will it come in, in this life?

I must not forget to tell you that our old lady friend, Mrs. Johnson3 died last Wednesday, at the age of 95 years & 4 months! Of old age—the doctor said she had no disease.

I send you, dear Walt a paper that that I promised, with notice of William's funeral. Send a postal card to let me know get them, please.

I am alone still, but am to have two young girls with me for six weeks, to board until their schools [ done.?]

One reason why I have so desired to hold on to a home, is that in the pecuniary distress of the Channings4 if they have to give up their beautiful home in Cal. I may have a refuge for them here, & so perhaps return in small measure their many benefits to us in the time of dear Jeannie's5 sickness, & again when William went out there. But, dear Walt, I try to keep up a good heart, & not doubt or dispond.

God bless you! with all the old love—

Nelly O'Connor.

Correspondent:
Ellen M. "Nelly" O'Connor was the wife of William D. O'Connor (1832–1889), one of Whitman's staunchest defenders. Whitman dined with the O'Connors frequently during his Washington years, and he speaks often in his letters of their daughter Jean, by nickname "Jenny" or "Jeannie." Though Whitman and William O'Connor would break in late 1872 over Reconstruction policies with regard to emancipated black citizens, Ellen would remain friendly with Whitman. The correspondence between Whitman and Ellen is almost as voluminous as the poet's correspondence with William. For more on Whitman's relationship with the O'Connors, see also Dashae E. Lott, "William Douglas O'Connor," Walt Whitman: An Encyclopedia, J.R. LeMaster and Donald D. Kummings, ed., (New York: Garland Publishing, 1998).

Notes:

1. This letter is addressed: Walt Whitman, | Camden, | New Jersey.. It is postmarked: Washington D.C. | APR 27 | 730 PM | 90; Camden N.J. | APR | 28 | 6am | 1890 | REC'D.[back]

2. William Douglas O'Connor (1832–1889) was the author of the grand and grandiloquent Whitman pamphlet "The Good Gray Poet," published in 1866 (a digital version of the pamphlet is available at "The Good Gray Poet: A Vindication"). For more on Whitman's relationship with O'Connor, see Deshae E. Lott, "O'Connor, William Douglas (1832–1889)," Walt Whitman: An Encyclopedia, ed. J.R. LeMaster and Donald D. Kummings (New York: Garland Publishing, 1998).[back]

3. Nancy M. Johnson, listed in the 1875 Directory as a widow. Whitman sent a set of books to her, as mentioned in his letter to Ellen O'Connor.[back]

4. William F. Channing (1820–1901), son of William Ellery Channing, and also Ellen O'Connor's brother-in-law, was by training a doctor, but devoted most of his life to scientific experiments. With Moses G. Farmer, he perfected the first fire-alarm system. He was the author of Notes on the Medical Applications of Electricity (Boston: Daniel Davis, Jr., and Joseph M. Wightman, 1849). Ellen O'Connor visited him frequently in Providence, Rhode Island, and Whitman stayed at his home in October, 1868.[back]

5. Jean was the daughter of William D. and Ellen O'Connor. William speaks often in his letters of Jean, calling her by her nicknames of "Jenny" or "Jeannie."[back]
