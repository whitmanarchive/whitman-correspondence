Washington, D.C.,
April 17 1890.



Mr. Walt Whitman,

My dear Sir:

I am preparing a series of letters for the above newspapers on How to Reach Three Score and Ten Years of Age and I hope to have information on this subject from Hon. W.E. Gladstone1, Hon. Ferdinand de [ Leeseps?] 2, Hon. George Bancroft3, Hon. Hamilton Fish4, Mr. Oliver Wendell Holmes5, Hon. John G. Whittier6 and other distinguished octogenarians. Will you please let me know:

As to what you attribute your good health through life.

As to your daily habits of work and exercise, sleep and diet.

Also what advice would you give to the young men of the day who wish to follow in your footsteps.

I would be glad to hear whether you are still working and also whether you believe in the use of spirituous liquors, tobacco, tea or coffee.

Also do you believe that marriage conduces to long life, and anything else you may wish to add of advice to a young man who wishes to reach three score and ten and pack the whole of his years with good work as you have done.

During what years did you do your best work and how long in your opinion should be the working life of man. I am

Very respectfully yours,

Frank G Carpenter.

I inclose stamped envelope for reply.

Correspondent:
As yet we have no information about this correspondent.

Notes:

1. As yet we have no information about this correspondent.[back]

2. As yet we have no information about this correspondent.[back]

3. George Bancroft (1800-1891), American diplomat in Europe and historian.[back]

4. George Bancroft (1800-1891), American diplomat in Europe and historian.[back]

5. Oliver Wendell Holmes, Sr. (1809–1894) was a Bostonian author, physician, and lecturer. One of the Fireside Poets, he was a good friend of Ralph Waldo Emerson as well as John Burroughs. Towards Whitman's poetry, Holmes remained ambivalent. He married Amelia Lee Jackson in 1840 and they had three children, including the later Supreme Court judge Oliver Wendell Holmes, Jr. For more information, see "Holmes, Oliver Wendell (1809-1894)," (Walt Whitman: An Encyclopedia, eds. J.R. LeMaster and Donald D. Kummings [New York: Garland Publishing, 1998], 280).[back]

6. John Greenleaf Whittier (1807–1892) earned fame as a staunch advocate for the abolition of slavery. As a poet, he employed traditional forms and meters, and, not surprisingly, he was not an admirer of Whitman's unconventional prosody. For Whitman's view of Whittier, see the poet's numerous comments throughout the nine volumes of Horace Traubel's With Walt Whitman in Camden (various publishers: 1906–1996) and Whitman's "My Tribute to Four Poets," in Specimen Days (Philadelphia: Rees Welsh & Co., 1882–'83), 180–181.[back]
