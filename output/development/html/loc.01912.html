[ Ovrenin Gausdal?] ,
28 July 1874.



Dear Walt Whitman,

I write to you these lines from old Norway. I am dwelling here in Gausdal in one of these ancient farms, whose names are perhaps thousand years old. I am walking among these heights—when the great mountains [illegible] [ are scattered?] over the [ scanty earth?] and laying on the same place as when for 5-6 hundred years ago the fierce chiefs met with their yeomen and retainers in bloody battles. At present all is very peaceful. The red blood-hours are spread over the whole valley, and good roads have been cut among the [ dark?] firs and pines. The river is running through the bottom as a smiling [ child?] . Sometimes it is white and [ reeking?] with foam as an injured ghost and for two weeks ago it took ago a new bridge as early as I am flowing a feather away with the breath of my mouth.

The people is good [ natured?] , vigorous and kind hearted. The peasants are still [ here saying?] , [ "Thou"?] to the strangers. A great deal of Englishmen and some Americans are travelling here in Norway.

The editor of "Aftenbladet" in Christiania told me, that he [ has?] forwarded one copy of Elsters criticism to you. I sent another from Copenhagen.

I have thought, that it could amaze you to receive some lines from this old proud mountain-country. Don't forget to let me know, if they have duely reached you.



—Rudolf Schmidt

Correspondent:
The Swede Peter Carl Rudolph Schmidt (1836–1899) was the editor of the idealist For Idé og Virkelighed ("For Idea and Reality") and had translated Whitman's Democratic Vistas into Danish in 1871 (see the letter from Schmidt to Whitman of ).
