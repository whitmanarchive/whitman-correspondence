12 Well Rd
Hampstead, London
England
1888
June
16th

———

I feel very sad about dear old Walts illness.

The two telegrams in the London "Daily News" are so alarming—I fear I shall never see him again; I've half a mind to start off now to Philadelphia.

If you see Walt again in this world after receiving this letter, give him my love.

Write to me as soon as you get this if it's only a few lines.

Tell me about your own health, how much work are you doing every day. Can I be of use? I wish I could be of more comfort to you.

I am coming to America about the middle of August.

I hope that you are not working just the same as you did last summer. You must remember what the doctors told you.

It will not do for you to be ill again. I feel horribly anxious about your health!

Let me know if you require anything. I hope it will not be very hot in America in August. What I recollect of it last year, was that it began to "let up" in August cool nights, and that sort of thing.

Mrs. Pearsall1 has told me of a nice little studio in Chestnut street high up that is only about $75 a year, which would just suit me. My idea is to hire a studio & one other room in Chestnut street, & to get my meals out at different cheap restaurants I, dare say, I could get a meal for 25 cents, and in the colder weather I could cook my own breakfast or at any rate my tea on my studio stove? So I shall bring a few cooking utensils & a few other things. I want to make drawings of street scenes in Philadelphia and during the election in November—and maybe, also, that I shall travel to other cities a little during the elections. It is possible, also, that I may get work to do in connection with [ in?] :—Cherry street Academy of Fine Arts, but of that I am not certain.

I am getting on with my portrait of Mrs. Costelloe2; I reckon to finish it before I leave England, and to leave it behind all ready for next year's Govenor of Royal Academy.

She is a light hearted fine lady, talks to me in a lively strain during the sitting, but she is so superficial, every time one dips the oar of conversation in, I seem to scrape the sandy bottom!! And would you believe, that Westminster is five good miles from our house and at 2 O.C. she leaves me to get home as best I can, & to get a lunch where I can; there is no inhospitality like that of a fine lady from Germantown, however, no one will know when they look at her blooming cheeks on my canvas that the artist painted it on an empty stomach; at least so my sister laughs, and says.

The Smiths, who have just arrived from Philadelphia tell me that Dr. Bucke3 procured Walt a man nurse; which was just what the dear old fellow wanted, I was so glad to hear it.

Of my two friends across the pond no one knows how much I think and feel & how little I say. I haven't written to Walt yet since his illness for I thought he might be too ill to open my letter. I have just received your second letter dated June 14th4 as well as the former in wch you spoke of your great loss. I received the former a week or 2 weeks ago. My dear friend, are you going through this hot summer without a servant?—and doing all the work yourself? Tell me; and tell me have you a return of any of your old ailments because the doctors will shake their heads if you have—you must be careful.

I hope that I shall be able to be some comfort to my friends during my visit to the States.

I can't very well manage to write a [illegible] family letter this time as I want this to catch this afternoon's mail. Mrs. Costelloe's portrait is coming on, and I think that it will do me credit & do think that I may see Walt yet in the flesh. I do rejoice in his recovery; Debbie gives very hopeful account of him, at wch I am so pleased.

Answer my question Madam, do you want anything?

It has been quit hot here: so hot that I lay awake all last night. I am so glad to hear that [ Mr. S's?] 5 strawberries promise so well. We have had lots of rain. Where has Van gone?

Grace6 is going to give a large party 75 people here next Saturday—week probably the day that you will receive this, between 8:30 and 12:30 in the evening.

———



Herb.

Tell me more about your health and spirits? I can't think how Ed7 & Mr S can work out in the blazing sun?

Correspondent:
Herbert Harlakenden Gilchrist (1857–1914), son of Alexander and Anne Gilchrist, was an English painter and editor of Anne Gilchrist: Her Life and Writings (London: T. Fisher Unwin, 1887). For more information, see Marion Walker Alcaro, "Gilchrist, Herbert Harlakenden (1857–1914)," Walt Whitman: An Encyclopedia, ed. J.R. LeMaster and Donald D. Kummings (New York: Garland Publishing, 1998).

Notes:

1. Robert Pearsall Smith (1827–1898) was a Quaker who became an evangelical minister associated with the "Holiness movement." He was also a writer and businessman. Whitman often stayed at his Philadelphia home, where the poet became friendly with the Smith children—Mary, Logan, and Alys. For more information about Smith, see Christina Davey, "Smith, Robert Pearsall (1827–1898)," Walt Whitman: An Encyclopedia, ed. J.R. LeMaster and Donald D. Kummings (New York: Garland Publishing, 1998).[back]

2. Mary Whitall Smith Costelloe (1864–1945) was a political activist, art historian, and critic, whom Whitman once called his "staunchest living woman friend." A scholar of Italian Renaissance art and a daughter of Robert Pearsall Smith, she would in 1885 marry B. F. C. "Frank" Costelloe. She had been in contact with many of Whitman's English friends and would travel to Britain in 1885 to visit many of them, including Anne Gilchrist shortly before her death. For more, see Christina Davey, "Costelloe, Mary Whitall Smith (1864–1945)," Walt Whitman: An Encyclopedia, ed. J.R. LeMaster and Donald D. Kummings (New York: Garland Publishing, 1998).[back]

3. Richard Maurice Bucke (1837–1902) was a Canadian physician and psychiatrist who grew close to Whitman after reading Leaves of Grass in 1867 (and later memorizing it) and meeting the poet in Camden a decade later. Even before meeting Whitman, Bucke claimed in 1872 that a reading of Leaves of Grass led him to experience "cosmic consciousness" and an overwhelming sense of epiphany. Bucke became the poet's first biographer with Walt Whitman (Philadelphia: David McKay, 1883), and he later served as one of his medical advisors and literary executors. For more on the relationship of Bucke and Whitman, see Howard Nelson, "Bucke, Richard Maurice," Walt Whitman: An Encyclopedia, ed. J.R. LeMaster and Donald D. Kummings (New York: Garland Publishing, 1998).[back]

4. The letter is apparently lost.[back]

5. Susan M. Stafford was the mother of Harry Stafford, who, in 1876, became a close friend of Whitman while working at the printing office of the Camden New Republic. Whitman regularly visited the Staffords at their family farm near Kirkwood, New Jersey. Whitman enjoyed the atmosphere and tranquility that the farm provided and would often stay for weeks at a time (see David G. Miller, "Stafford, George and Susan M.," Walt Whitman: An Encyclopedia, ed. J.R. LeMaster and Donald D. Kummings [New York: Garland Publishing, 1998], 685).[back]

6. Grace Gilchrist Frend (1859–1947) was one of Anne Gilchrist's four children and Herbert's sister. She became a contralto. She was the author of "Walt Whitman as I Remember Him" (Bookman 72 [July 1927], 203–205).[back]

7. Edwin Stafford (1856–1906) was one of Susan Stafford's sons.[back]
