Morristown N.J.
Aug. 31/69



My dear Walt Whitman,

I have the advantage of you—I know you well—"My soul embraces you this hour, and we affect each-other without ever seeing each other, and never perhaps to see each other"—I hope not though.

[ Good Summer?] , and this I have been living in the woods, with "leaves of Grass" beside me, me of my rustic [ sympathies?] .

And now that the days are growing cold I will have to content myself indoors. I don't like to—but must soon say good bye to the [ Grass?] here by the brook—               Many a time while lying on the Bank of the little stream reading your Book—have I wondered just what manner of man is Walt Whitman?—and I have thought of him so much that I seem to know him well—I hope some of these days I may have the pleasure of a visit from him—

I am spending my summer very simply in this place—and should be most happy to have Mr. Whitman should a Sunday with me when he can make it convenient to do so—I go to [ Lome?] , Mass.[ &e?] next week, for a change—but will be at home in a few weeks—and will be pleased to see you at any time—

Hope you will pardon my intrusion, but I am anxious to know you better, and would call you friend—

Would be pleased to hear from you at any time—

Sincerely Yours,

Meredith R. Brookfield
