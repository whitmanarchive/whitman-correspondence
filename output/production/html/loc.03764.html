  57 Pembroke St. Toronto, Ont. Feby 1st 1890. My dear Sir.

There lies before me, as I write, a copy of "Brother Johnathan" Vol 1. January 29th, 1842. No 5. The first article is a piece of poetry:

Written for the Brother Jonathan—

Ambition

By Walter Whitman.

One day, an obscure youth, a wanderer, Known but to few, lay musing with himself About the chances of his future life. etc: etc:

I would be very glad to hear from  you, as to whether this was one of your early poems. & if you have it in rememberance.

I have been a rambler in these old periodicals, magazines: etc: of 50 or 60 years ago, & found very many interesing pieces by our American Poets: notably some of Longfellow (1826), O.Wendell Holmes, (1829) etc:—

I was much pleased to read the account in a late "New York Times" & very glad to hear you "rest a good deal, try to worry about nothing, and don't think too much." good. good. good—

What an interesting fact came  to light the other day, that the only Sister of Keats, passed over to the other side last December,.

She was living at Madrid: & died aged about 86, leaving children and grandchildren.

A friend of mine, Mr. W. J. Linton, is I think an old acquaintence of yours—I have heard him talk of you often:—he has been in England the last two years, preparing a great work on Engraving.—& purposes I believe, very soon to return to America.—

Permit me to wish you the best things, and anyway "The storms of Life & Wintry Time will quickly pass, and one unbounded Spring Encircle all."

  Cordially Yours Geo. E. Sears. Walt Whitman: Camden.
